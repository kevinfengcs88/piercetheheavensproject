Cognitive Robotics – An Introduction

Posted October 1, 2019October 14, 2019 Kevin Feng

The computer has become a part of our everyday lives. Without it, simple tasks become onerous ones.

But what does a computer mean in the name of human innovation? Certainly it is an effective invention; it makes simple calculations seemingly instantaneous, it turns rigorous manual labor into automation, and it makes information accessible with a click of a button.

The computer has even found its way into our hands, ready at our fingertips to give us whatever knowledge we desire; we can contact anyone at anytime.

However, with sentient beings creating entities designed for intelligent behavior comes the prospect of them gaining their own cognition. But where is that line that divides the computer, a machine, from the sentient entity of artificial intelligence? Is it when it is able to feel pain? Will experiencing emotion incur a sudden revelation? When it decides to create art, is humanity doomed?

There are so many questions to ask with such little time. Arguably, artificial intelligence is already here: in our devices in the form of audio input based user interfaces like Siri or Alexa. These programs that have been dubbed with names (further bridging the gap that we perceive for sentient robots) answer our questions, execute commands, and even provide surprisingly human personality to our lives. Still, everything that is accomplished by these forms of “artificial intelligence” is from a link to the Internet, to other computers. Nothing that is done by Siri is self-reliant. What Siri says, or does, it nothing but a function of a computer, albeit, very complex.

The point at which a computer becomes self-aware, then, is not so clear. We can make predictions and we can guess as to how it will happen, but only one thing is certain: The rise of another sentient species will bring a new philosophy to the world.

The study of cognitive robotics isn’t something new, even if it is revolutionary. In fact, the simple function of logic was introduced into AI as early as 1958. Artificial intelligence itself has been a persistently advancing technology since John McCarthy coined the term in 1956. Decades later, simple, but prevalent forms of artificial intelligence have become a part of everyone’s lives.

So what is the difference between cognitive robotics and artificial intelligence?

Well in practicality, there is virtually no difference. The rise of one of the two deeply intertwined technologies will most certainly spell a synchronous and analogous rise in the other. But cognitive robotics does imply a slightly different connotation from that of the general term of “artificial” intelligence. For robotics are something that mankind has created in the hopes of greater efficiency in the tangible, real world (which it has done), while artificial intelligence can include that, as well as a general expansion in computational power across the topological map of technology.

Robots, when they are sentient, are the realization of the potential that artificial intelligence as a concept holds. Without the real life interaction with a robot, or some kind of tangible body to contain its brain (no longer a CPU), human recognition of emotional awareness becomes distorted, or refracted, like a straw in a glass of water.

But the straw is not something to fool us forever. The slight delay caused by absence is only one of the three components that AI has the potential to possess. The first is the Mind, which is the very essence of primitive forms of artificial intelligence. The ability to learn and the ability to use logic are strengths and already common in computers today. There is almost no concern regarding the presence of Mind in these sentient machines.

The Body is the delay caused without a tangible interaction with the physical world. It is by no means an impasse, as the solution is what cognitive robotics implies.

The Soul is what must be questioned.
